Commemorative Plaques Record Details

George Davidsone (unknown - 1663)

George Davidsone's plaque in St Clement's Churchyard

This is his grave stone in St Nicholas Churchyard.

Location : West wall, St Clement's Churchyard, St Clements Street

Area : Central Aberdeen

Plaque Type :Non-standard / Memorial

About George Davidsone :Merchant and patron. His career began as an itinerant drapery salesman. He was extremely successful, becoming a wealthy merchant and landowner. He was a great benefactor of the church: he mortified lands and money for the erection of a chapel in Newhills and for the maintenance of the minister there. He was also a great benefactor of St Clement’s Church in Fittie, where one of his acts, the erection of a no longer extant boundary dyke is commemorated by this plaque.

More Information : The details of his early life are not known. He began as a chapman, or packman an itinerant drapery salesman. His career was very successful and he ended up a rich merchant trading in grain and acting as a banker. He became a substantial landowner in and around Aberdeen purchasing the estates of, amongst others, Bogfairley and Cepplehills (Newhills). He was admitted as a burgess of Aberdeen on 8 June 1626. He was a great benefactor of the church: he mortified the lands of Bogfairley and Newhills to support the church and minister in Newhills. In 1662 he began the erection of Newhills parish church. He died before the church was completed and is buried in St Nicholas churchyard, where there remains an impressive carved stone monument. He also caused a bow bridge to be built over the Bucksburn that runs through Newhills parish, it having been said that he had witnessed a man drown there and vowed to see a bridge built to avert any future tragedy. He also repaired the bridge at Insch (when admitted as a burgess in 1626 he was ‘duelling at the church of Insh…’). His travels as an itinerant salesman must have made apparent to him the poor state of roads and bridges in the hinterland of Aberdeen at that time; clearly he was a man of conviction. This plaque was erected in 1650 in order to commemorate his endeavour to enclose St Clement’s churchyard with a dyke, St Clement’s was the parish church of the Fittie, or Footdee, area of Aberdeen. In 1631 he was one of several people who subscribed money to the reinstatement of St Clement’s, which had fallen into disuse and decay following the Reformation. He also bequeathed monies both for the poor of Fittie and a separate bequest for the upkeep of the dyke. It would seem that the dyke he caused to be built was probably demolished in 1787; according to the historian Kennedy the plaque was removed and built into a new wall when the church was rebuilt in 1787. On this plaque is a coat of arms. These are unusual and it has been suggested that they are the arms of (or relate to the arms of) the Cairnbrogie family, from which family it has been said Davidsone was descended. However as the details of his early life are lost there is no way to prove this family connection. Moreover the arms are not those of the Cairnbrogie family. In fact there is no record of a grant of arms ever having been made to Davidsone, thus he may not have had any rights to a coat of arms. Perhaps these arms are testimony to the way that Davidsone wanted to see himself in society. Curiously on his elaborate memorial in St Nicholas churchyard there is an empty niche where his arms (if he had any) ought to be. In 1979 a replacement plaque had to be commissioned as the original had badly deteriorated; it remains very possible that the plaque, which crumbled when it was removed, (in 1979) may have been the 1650 original. There was also a memorial to Davidsone in Newhills church, erected in 1663 and repaired by the minister in 1680. A memorial pew in that church also commemorated him, the original 1663 Newhills church survives now only as a ruin. There is a small, rusted, plaque in the internal wall of the remains Newhills church erected by Aberdeen District Council in the 1930s to the memory of Davidsone.
